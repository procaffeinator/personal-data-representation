A 10-Day Digital Detox Journey

Detox V/s Non-Detox Screen Time Comparison

Initial Concept

I began by consolidating data from the last two months, specifically Jan-Feb 2024, to understand how my sleep hours and screen time vary according to the phase of my menstrual cycle. Through this exploration, I aimed to find correlations between these variables and gain insights into my digital habits and overall well-being.

Data Collection

For my data collection process, I utilized various sources to gather information on:

{Date, Day, Screen Time, Phase of Menstrual Cycle, Phase of Detox Cycle, Top 3 Apps}

To track Screen Time, I relied on iPhone settings, which provided daily insights into my screen usage. Extracting details on the Top 3 Apps was straightforward from iPhone settings, identifying my most frequently used applications. For monitoring menstrual cycle phases, I used a menstrual tracker app (PC) to log and classify different stages. The experiment consisted of two distinct phases: the initial 10 days were non-detox, while the subsequent 10 days were detox. I compiled all of this data in Google Sheets.

Data Feminism

As I delved deeper into my data analysis, I recognized the importance of tracing back to the context surrounding the data. Incorporating menstrual cycle data into my analysis fostered a more inclusive understanding of menstrual health and its impact on my digital usage. This approach, rooted in data feminism [3], helped me comprehend and visualize the intricate relationship between my menstrual cycle and digital habits.

Design Transition (Design Iterations)

I noticed through my initial data analysis that I spend a lot of time mindlessly scrolling through Instagram. I decided to do a detox experiment to compare screen time across a 20-day window, divided into 10 days of non-detox and 10 days of detox, where I uninstalled Instagram. This detox aimed to regulate my digital usage and monitor my digital footprint more effectively. During this period, I noticed a shift in my app usage, particularly towards productivity apps like LinkedIn and Outlook, reflecting my job search activities. Despite the detox, I maintained regular usage of WhatsApp, my primary mode of communication with family in India, but with regulated usage.

Refining the Design

In refining my design process, I opted for simplicity and clarity. I used a bubble chart to visualize the correlation between sleep hours and screen time. On further thought, I deciding to omit sleep hours due as they were mostly constant and not affected by any of the factors under consideration. I ditched the bubble chart and continued to explore other ways of visualizing this data.

I tried out visualizing this data using a Stacked Bar Chart, Line Chart, Steamgraph etc to find the best fit for my dataset. For the final design, I chose a polar area chart as it offered a comprehensive day-by-day comparison of screen time, with the area size representing usage intensity. This unconventional format facilitated a nuanced understanding of my digital habits across different menstrual phases. By color-coding the phases of the menstrual cycle, I could visually observe variations in my usage patterns. I chose warm colors like red, orange, and yellow to represent menstrual phases. This departure from traditional color associations fosters a more inclusive narrative around menstrual health, encouraging open dialogue and understanding. This is useful as I have borderline PMS and it helps me uncover relationships between my menstrual cycle and digital footprint. I also decided to add an option to view the top 3 apps allowed me to regulate my app usage and track my digital activities more efficiently.

Design Medium

Incorporating chart.js as my design medium, I leveraged its flexibility and functionality to plot and visualize my data effectively. This medium allowed me to explore unconventional representations of my data and challenge traditional visualization practices, aligning with the principles of data feminism and inclusive data analysis.
